Biases That Affect Evaluations

2017-12-19

Both formally and informally, we all have to perform evaluations on a daily basis. Whether it is doing someone's performance evaluation at work or choosing which library to use for a given problem, we can't escape it. More often than not, we make errors in this due to the biases and heuristics we have learned to have. I will try to list some of those here.

I won't try to list all types of thinking errors. There was something of a masterlist compiled in a book by Rolf Dobelli called "The Art Of Thinking Clearly,". That book is what bible would read like if it was written today. If Sun Tzu was from today's era, he would have had a chapter on it in The Art Of War. It is a must read for everyone. It just so happens that I am reading through a HR book and I am trying to reconcile what I am learning with my past experience by reflecting on it here. So here goes:

Halo Effect

The other day our CEO gave his routine end of year Christmas speech. He was dressed very informally, grew a bit of a beard, was in a check shirt with some washed up jeans. Seeing him made a weird thought run through my head: Why did he dress like a hipster for this particular occasion?

I soon remembered that halo effect was at work. If it was a different industry, the CEO would have been right to wear an expensive suit. But, well, I work in the topmost ICT R&D organisation in the country and almost everyone in the company dresses like that. Positive things usually aren't associated with dressing up. As far as I recall, there was only once when I was asked to dress sharply. It was during a public marketing event where one of my projects were being presented. Before that, the only time I dressed up was during my job interview (being ex public service) and during my first day at work (never again).

Halo effect is our tendency to generalise domain specific competency across domains. In above instance, for example, people's fashion sense is being seen as an indicator of their competency. People usually associate a variety of factors to a variety of competencies. For example, tallness, attractiveness, what kind of car they drive etc. They do this because they don't know you and because they are lazy. So they set their expectation based on things that are easy to derive.

The way to not be influenced by halo effect while giving evaluations is to establish clear performance evaluation criteria beforehand and sticking to it. Being conscious of it helps. However, there is still a probability that your your judgement in those criteria are being swayed.

Horn Effect

Horn effect is the exact opposite of Halo effect where negative attributes of individuals are taken as an indicator of unrelated competencies negatively. For example, person X does not like D&D so he must not be a good engineer. These are just as stupid as Halo Effects.

We are influenced by these biases because these heuristics usually serve us. For example, food that smell bad are usually rotten and taste bad. However, we also need to keep in mind that they are just heuristics. If not, for example, we would never be able to taste great tasting food like Durian and Papaya if we strictly adhered by those rules. Once again, being aware of it, being realistic about it and sticking to a strict set of criteria may help mitigate some of this.

Social Proof

This is another one of those heuristics that serve us. For example, we typically use libraries and technology that a lot of other people use or say are good. So we expect it to be good and we are usually satisfied with it either due to the product or the person either being good or due to self fulfilment prophecy/confirmation bias. This is why we hire consultants, follow thought leaders and go to conferences etc. They tell us things that are safe and proven.

I am not saying that we should not do these things. They provide a lot of value. I could call someone who did not exploit existing wisdom to maximise his chances a fool. What I have problems with is only following established practice and never experimenting. These will usually serve you well until when you have to step outside your me too box. For example, when you come up with something exceptional or revolutionary or innovative etc. These will only slow you down then.

In the tech sector, there are additional things like textbook examples, accepted practice, "standard practice", COTS software etc which work similar to social proof. For example. when was the last time you built a system that was not a database backed three layered system? Sometimes, there are "my way or the high way" people that will penalise you for what they perceive to be the best solution or, worse, the only way to solve a problem. It is worse when it is done retrospectively. "That was a bad decision. Why did you do things this way? It is not standard practice." If you have been practising in the industry a while and have ever tried new things, chances are that you've heard phrases similar to this. Retrospective distortion. No! They don't get to have a say unless they were there originally and spoke for things to be otherwise.

In machine learning, there is the concept of balancing exploration and exploitation. I think that is something that should apply universally to professional practice. When you are giving evaluations, be aware of not dismissing new or different things and points of view. When you are in teams with conservative and political people, be a little bit move a little bit slowly. Be sure to frame things properly and keep them informed etc.

Confirmation Bias

This is one of the worst ones.

Suppose that I referred you to John and said that he is very friendly. Suppose that in your first interaction with him, he acts in an ambiguous manner that could be interpreted to be friendly or anything else. Unless he explicitly expressed hostility, you will most likely interpret that as a signal to be friendly and it will reinforce your expectation that he is friendly. What would happen if I told you instead that he was a little bit crazy.

Few years ago, I came to think that people who write software for the JVM were not very intelligent. How? I hung out with the wrong thought leaders. I respected their opinions and they made such opinion known to me. This is before I learned about confirmation bias and such. I, of course, have stopped building systems in java and to this date, I have not met anyone I'd respect to be intelligent who wrote software for JVM. Except maybe my first mentor. Well, he was also a polygot like me who programmed in whatever was necessary. In reality, whether I and them believe if JVM was trash or was the best platform out there is not of any consequence. The reason for that is that it is not a rule of nature. People who think that java is the best thing ever may live their entire life and satisfy their clients by programming in java. They are of no consequence to people who think java is absolutely rubbish. They also can satisfy their clients and live their entire life without even knowing about the existence of java. There are plenty of other alternatives. Same thing could be said about 90% of the technologies of today. Whether you like them or hate them is of no consequence. But if you are evaluating someone else's performance based on your inconsequential beliefs, it stops to be harmless.

More recently, someone told me that functional programming was the only way to produce correct, robust software. Academia driven bullshit of course. Spread by the type of idiots who would have you believe that the only reason we have wheels is because a scientist wrote a paper to point out the possibility of wheels. This person was also a thought leader. Someone I respected. Unfortunately, this was after I had learned about confirmation bias. Sure, I have learned a great deal about functional programming since then including doing formal courses. I wasn't gonna dismiss it. What if it was indeed the promised holy grail? But I was also humble about it. I knew that people said similar things about OOP before. There wasn't anything particularly difficult about it. It was just using a different set of concepts than the ones I was used to using. However, I have not yet stitched over to functional programming as I have not seen any value or benefit in the change. There isn't anything revolutionary in FP that a good static code checker combined with minimal testing can not overcompensate for. It is not a particularly interesting programming paradigm either. So far, staying true to things I value in my professional practice, such as seeing happy clients and users, has not required it. I like to think that I am practical. I would automate away writing code if I could. So far it is the most boring aspect of my job. That being said, I'd probably still use a functional programming language if there was a real need for it. For example, if I was writing code for a pacemaker or sending rockets into space. The overhead would be more than justified.

Anyways, be careful of the thoughts and opinions you have inside your head. Especially the ones you feel very strongly about. See how they influence how you evaluate others. Also, don't evaluate people negatively based on how other people might see them. Of course, bring it to their attention as part of their feedback if you are particularly concerned.

In terms of others being influenced by confirmation bias, there is not a lot you can do. Be positive, humble, friendly etc and maybe what goes around will come around. Even the nicest person in the world will have critiques.

Fundamental Attribution Error And Similar To Me Errors

People have a tendency to explain away other's behaviour to their attributes and their own behaviour to environmental factors. For example, if Mary forgot to write a test case, it is because she lacks attention to detail. But when I forget to write a test cases, it is always because I was in a rush at the time.

This works in inverse as well. When we do something successfully, we attribute it to our characteristics. The project performed beyond expectations because of my slick project management skills. When the same thing happens for someone else, it is usually due to luck or some other situational circumstances.

This also happens at large scales in groups. E.g. different race, particular tech stack users, alumni of different universities or ex colleagues of a particular company etc. Behaviour of members of other group gets explained away by their attributes and behaviours of members of our group to circumstances. This also happens to people and groups you put on pedestals. If you, for example, think that functional programmers are smarter, you will experience similar effects. If you think the opposite, you will also selectively focus on only negative aspects of that group.

Everyone does it! It is some kind of a natural defence mechanism. Unfortunately, it clouds our evaluations, stops our own selves from improving and stops us from having real relationships with people. It is something to be very weary of due to another issue called the recency error. Our brains rarely think comprehensively about anything. When we evaluate people, we will most likely focus on one or two events that stood out. These things that stand out are either very exceptional performance or negative things. Due to the way we process information, business as usual things usually don't stand out as much.

So, before highlighting away past events as negative attributes of other people, consider their circumstances. Chances are that you could not have done any better in the situation.

And if you want to stand out in your performance reviews, cause fake panics and take credit for resolving them. I am kidding. Don't! That would be unethical. But keep a list of your accomplishments as well as you will also forget. Something like a learning log or an engineers diary can be invaluable during reviews and things.

Another way this goes wrong is when people are put on pedestals. For example, maybe there is a rock star hire or emerging employee etc. People build up certain expectations of them. Things usually work out with help from conformation bias. However, if they mess up enough or badly, the fall breaks them inside your mind and all their negative aspects get highlighted. Another fun effect of putting people on pedestals is the phenomenon where for one reason or another, you won't be able to outperform them. So don't put people on pedestals. Everyone are victims of their circumstances or no one is.

Recency Errors, Primacy Errors and Contrast Errors

Unless things stand out, evaluations are often done in light of the most recent set of events and interactions that are fresh in mind. It is worse when a member or the team or something particularly stands out during a recent crisis or something. All the evaluator can think of is that event and that person and everyone else gets unhelpful mediocre evaluations.

This happens because our brains are incapable of thinking comprehensively about things. Thinking is a very slow process. It is natural to forget things over time and you will never remember everything you need to make give good comprehensive evaluations.

What is the cure? If you are in a position where you know that you will have to give evaluations and feedback, take notes and use checklists throughout their performance. If you can't, you should not giving them evaluations.

Leniency Errors, Strictness Error, Error Of Central Tendency

These are errors where the evaluator has tendencies to either positively or negatively evaluate everyone. Remember that professor that gave everyone a B-?

Unfortunately, I've gone through the entire list. In my first job, my manager was too lenient. He was a non-techie manager who only gave positive feedback. During one of my performance reviews, I demanded a mentor (and got it :D). I recently had a client who was too strict and gave evaluations mostly in negatives. He'd always highlight everything that is wrong with your project. No word of thanks or compliments when you fixed them. In a past sprint, when a complement came out of his mouth, the entire team synchronously exclaimed "WHAT DID HE JUST SAY?." That was a first! My last manager had central tendencies and evaluated everyone in the team to be average. If you screwed up recently, you want to be below average. Upon reflection, I think he was a bit too conservative.

To avoid this, you need to come up with some kind of strategy. If you evaluate most people in the negative or average, there probably something wrong with your personality. If you evaluate everyone positively all the time, you are not helping them grow. So you need to come up with some kind of strategy. For example, some people scale.

I personally like to follow a barbel strategy. In terms of giving feedback, I pick 1-3 things that really stood out about their performance and 1-2 sticking points less than or equal to the number of things that stood out. Rest is small talk/filler words. Of course, everyone always does not get to win. For example, where you have been asked to judge at contests. You will sometimes have to rank people. Scoring can work but is time consuming. I have recently found sorting to be an effective strategy in that where I do pairwise comparisons based on criteria sections and sort people until I reach an ordering that I think is fair.

Happy evaluatons.
